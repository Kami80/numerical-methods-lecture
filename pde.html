PDE Classification FoundationDecision Guide

Before choosing a numerical method, identify the mathematical type of the PDE, the required initial and boundary data, and whether the solution evolves in time or represents a steady field. Choosing the wrong scheme for the wrong PDE type is the most common source of divergence and unphysical results.

General second-order linear PDE in two variables
\[ A\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} +B\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x\partial y} +C\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} +D\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} +E\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} +Fu=G \]

The classification depends only on the highest-order coefficients through the discriminant:

\[ \Delta = B^2-4AC \]
TypeConditionCanonical equationPhysical meaningData neededCommon numerical methods
Elliptic\(B^2-4AC<0\)\(u_{xx}+u_{yy}=0\)Steady conduction, potential flow, electrostaticsBCs on a closed boundaryFDM, Gauss-Seidel, SOR/PSOR, FEM
Parabolic\(B^2-4AC=0\)\(u_t=\alpha u_{xx}\)Diffusion, unsteady heat and mass transferOne IC + BCsFTCS, BTCS, Crank-Nicolson, ADI, MOL
Hyperbolic\(B^2-4AC>0\)\(u_{tt}=c^2u_{xx}\)Waves, vibrations, acoustics, water hammerTwo ICs + BCsCentral difference, Lax-Wendroff, characteristics
📘
Worked Example — Classify Three PDEs by the Discriminant
1Classify \(3u_{xx}+4u_{xy}+u_{yy}=0\)

Identify the coefficients of the highest derivatives:

\(A=3,\qquad B=4,\qquad C=1\)
\[ \Delta=B^2-4AC=16-12=4 \]
\(\Delta=4>0\Rightarrow\) Hyperbolic. Wave-type methods with a CFL restriction are required.
2Classify \(u_{xx}+2u_{xy}+u_{yy}+u_x=0\)
\(A=1,\qquad B=2,\qquad C=1\)
\[ \Delta=4-4=0 \]
\(\Delta=0\Rightarrow\) Parabolic. First-order terms do not change the type — only \(A,B,C\) matter.
3Classify \(u_{xx}+u_{yy}+5u=10\)
\(A=1,\qquad B=0,\qquad C=1\)
\[ \Delta=0-4=-4 \]
\(\Delta=-4<0\Rightarrow\) Elliptic. This is a Helmholtz-type steady equation; iterative or direct linear solvers apply.

Boundary condition types

TypeMathematical formEngineering interpretationFDM treatment
Dirichlet\(u=a\)Specified temperature, concentration, displacementMove known value to RHS
Neumann\(\partial u/\partial n=q\)Specified heat flux or mass flux; \(q=0\) means insulatedGhost node + central difference
Robin\(a u+b\,\partial u/\partial n=c\)Convection boundary: \(-k\,T_x=h_c(T-T_\infty)\)Ghost node, eliminate with the BC
Initial condition: needed only when the PDE evolves in time. Parabolic PDEs need one IC, \(T(x,0)=T_0(x)\). Hyperbolic PDEs need two: \(u(x,0)\) and \(u_t(x,0)\), because \(u_{tt}\) is second order in time.
Boundary condition: required at every physical boundary of the domain. Elliptic problems need boundary data on the entire closed boundary — they have no marching direction.
Common mistake: trying to “march” an elliptic problem in one spatial direction like an initial value problem. Elliptic PDEs are globally coupled — every interior node feels every boundary — so they must be solved as a full linear system or by iteration until convergence.

Finite Difference Grid Setup Stencil Logic

The finite difference method replaces derivatives with algebraic approximations at grid nodes. This converts a PDE into a system of algebraic equations (steady problems) or a system of ODEs (transient problems). Everything downstream — accuracy, stability, cost — is decided here.

📐
Derivation — Where the Formulas Come From
1Taylor expansion of the neighbors

Expand \(u\) about node \(i\) with spacing \(h=\Delta x\):

\[ u_{i+1}=u_i+h\,u_i'+\frac{h^2}{2}u_i''+\frac{h^3}{6}u_i'''+\frac{h^4}{24}u_i''''+\cdots \] \[ u_{i-1}=u_i-h\,u_i'+\frac{h^2}{2}u_i''-\frac{h^3}{6}u_i'''+\frac{h^4}{24}u_i''''-\cdots \]
2Subtract for the first derivative, add for the second

Subtracting cancels all even-order terms:

\[ u_i'=\frac{u_{i+1}-u_{i-1}}{2h}-\frac{h^2}{6}u_i'''+\cdots \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad O(h^2) \]

Adding cancels all odd-order terms:

\[ u_i''=\frac{u_{i-1}-2u_i+u_{i+1}}{h^2}-\frac{h^2}{12}u_i''''+\cdots \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad O(h^2) \]
The leading error of the central second difference is \(-\dfrac{h^2}{12}u''''\). This is why central differences are exact for any polynomial up to degree 3 — the fourth derivative vanishes.
3Handling a Neumann boundary with a ghost node

Suppose the right boundary at node \(N\) is insulated: \(\left.\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial x}\right|_N=0\).

Introduce a fictitious ghost node \(T_{N+1}\) outside the domain and apply the central difference to the BC:

\[ \frac{T_{N+1}-T_{N-1}}{2h}=0 \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad T_{N+1}=T_{N-1} \]

Substituting into the interior stencil at node \(N\):

\[ \frac{T_{N-1}-2T_N+T_{N+1}}{h^2} =\frac{2T_{N-1}-2T_N}{h^2} \]
The ghost node disappears — the insulated boundary simply doubles the interior neighbor.
4Handling a convection (Robin) boundary

Convective cooling at the right face: \(-k\left.\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial x}\right|_N=h_c\,(T_N-T_\infty)\).

Central difference with a ghost node:

\[ \frac{T_{N+1}-T_{N-1}}{2h}=-\frac{h_c}{k}(T_N-T_\infty) \] \[ \Longrightarrow\quad T_{N+1}=T_{N-1}-\frac{2h\,h_c}{k}(T_N-T_\infty) \]

This expression is substituted into the node-\(N\) stencil, eliminating the ghost value and coupling \(T_N\) to the ambient temperature \(T_\infty\).

Forward difference — \(O(h)\)
\[u_x\big|_i\approx \frac{u_{i+1}-u_i}{\Delta x}\]
Backward difference — \(O(h)\)
\[u_x\big|_i\approx \frac{u_i-u_{i-1}}{\Delta x}\]
Central first derivative — \(O(h^2)\)
\[u_x\big|_i\approx \frac{u_{i+1}-u_{i-1}}{2\Delta x}\]
Central second derivative — \(O(h^2)\)
\[u_{xx}\big|_i\approx \frac{u_{i-1}-2u_i+u_{i+1}}{\Delta x^2}\]
Two-dimensional Laplacian, uniform grid
\[ \nabla^2 u\big|_{i,j} \approx \frac{u_{i-1,j}-2u_{i,j}+u_{i+1,j}}{\Delta x^2} + \frac{u_{i,j-1}-2u_{i,j}+u_{i,j+1}}{\Delta y^2} \]

For \(\Delta x=\Delta y=h\):

\[ \nabla^2u\approx\frac{u_E+u_W+u_N+u_S-4u_P}{h^2} \]
Halving \(h\) costs more than you think: in 2D, halving the spacing quadruples the number of unknowns; in a 2D transient problem with an explicit scheme it also forces \(\Delta t\) down by a factor of 4 — a total cost increase of 16×. This trade-off is exactly why implicit and ADI methods exist.

Laplace Equation Steady FieldLiebmann Iteration

Laplace's equation describes steady fields with no internal source. The discrete form says: the value at each interior node is the average of its four neighbors. Small grids can be solved exactly by hand; larger grids are solved by Liebmann (Gauss-Seidel) iteration or SOR.

Five-point Laplace update
\[ \frac{T_E+T_W+T_N+T_S-4T_P}{h^2}=0 \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad T_P=\frac{T_E+T_W+T_N+T_S}{4} \]

The coefficient matrix of the assembled system is symmetric and diagonally dominant (\(|{-4}|\ge 1+1+1+1\)), which guarantees Gauss-Seidel and SOR convergence.

🧊
Worked Example A — 2×2 Interior Plate (Exact Hand Solution)

Solve \(\nabla^2T=0\) on a square plate with four interior nodes. The top boundary is \(100^\circ C\); the left, right, and bottom boundaries are \(0^\circ C\).

1Write the four node equations

Let the bottom interior row be \(T_{11},T_{21}\) and the top interior row be \(T_{12},T_{22}\). Insert the known boundaries:

\[ \begin{aligned} 4T_{11}-T_{21}-T_{12}&=0\\ -T_{11}+4T_{21}-T_{22}&=0\\ -T_{11}+4T_{12}-T_{22}&=100\\ -T_{21}-T_{12}+4T_{22}&=100 \end{aligned} \]
2Exploit symmetry and solve

The left and right boundaries are identical, so \(T_{11}=T_{21}=a\) and \(T_{12}=T_{22}=b\).

Bottom equation: \(4a-a-b=0\Rightarrow 3a=b\)
Top equation: \(-a+4b-b=100\Rightarrow -a+3b=100\)
Substitute \(b=3a\): \(-a+9a=100\Rightarrow 8a=100\Rightarrow a=12.5\)
Therefore \(b=3(12.5)=37.5\)
3Verify by substituting back
Node \(T_{12}\): \(-12.5+4(37.5)-37.5=-12.5+150-37.5=100\;\checkmark\)
Node \(T_{22}\): \(-12.5-37.5+4(37.5)=-50+150=100\;\checkmark\)

Laplace equation by Liebmann iteration with over-relaxation
%% Laplace equation on a rectangular plate — Liebmann (Gauss-Seidel) + SOR
% PDE:  d2T/dx2 + d2T/dy2 = 0
% BCs:  top = 100, left = 75, right = 50, bottom = 0
% The interior update is the four-neighbor average, accelerated by omega.
clc; clear; close all;
%% Step 1: Grid and boundary data
Nx = 3; Ny = 3;              % interior nodes (use larger values for finer grids)
T  = zeros(Ny+2, Nx+2);      % full array including boundary rows/columns
T(end,:) = 100;              % top boundary
T(1,:)   = 0;                % bottom boundary
T(:,1)   = 75;               % left boundary
T(:,end) = 50;               % right boundary
%% Step 2: Iteration settings
omega   = 1.2;               % 1.0 = pure Gauss-Seidel
tol     = 1e-6;
maxIter = 10000;
%% Step 3: Liebmann sweeps
for iter = 1:maxIter
    T_old = T;
    for j = 2:Ny+1
        for i = 2:Nx+1
            T_gs   = 0.25*(T(j,i-1) + T(j,i+1) + T(j-1,i) + T(j+1,i));
            T(j,i) = (1-omega)*T(j,i) + omega*T_gs;
        end
    end
    err = max(abs(T(:) - T_old(:)));
    if err < tol
        break
    end
end
fprintf('Converged in %d sweeps, final change = %.2e\n', iter, err)
disp('Interior temperature field (top row printed first):')
disp(flipud(T(2:end-1, 2:end-1)))
%% Step 4: Visualization
figure;
contourf(T, 20); colorbar; axis equal tight;
title('Laplace Equation — Liebmann Iteration');

Poisson Equation Source Term

Poisson's equation adds an internal generation or consumption term. In heat transfer this is volumetric generation (electrical heating, reaction exotherm); in mass transfer it is a homogeneous reaction term.

Poisson update for \(\nabla^2T=-S\)
\[ \frac{T_E+T_W+T_N+T_S-4T_P}{h^2}=-S \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad T_P=\frac{T_E+T_W+T_N+T_S+h^2S}{4} \]

The only change relative to Laplace is the extra constant \(h^2S\) inside the average. Every solver from Section 03 works unchanged.

🔥
Worked Example A — Uniform Source, Symmetric Grid

Solve \(\nabla^2T=-36\) on a unit square with all boundaries fixed at zero. Use 2 interior nodes per direction, so \(h=1/3\).

1Compute the source contribution
\[ h^2S=\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^2(36)=\frac{36}{9}=4 \]

Each interior equation becomes \(4T_P-T_E-T_W-T_N-T_S=4\).

2Use symmetry and solve

All four interior nodes are equivalent by symmetry, so \(T_P=a\). Each node has two boundary neighbors (value 0) and two interior neighbors (value \(a\)):

\(4a-a-a-0-0=4\)
\(2a=4\Rightarrow \boxed{a=2}\)
The source lifts the interior above the cold boundary. Without the source the solution would be identically zero — the boundary alone provides no driving force.
⚗️

▶ MATLAB Code
Poisson equation — direct sparse assembly and solve
%% Poisson equation on a square plate:  del2(T) = -S
% Assembles the full sparse system A*T = b and solves it directly.
clc; clear; close all;
%% Step 1: Grid and physical data
N = 20;                       % interior nodes per direction
L = 1;
h = L/(N+1);
S = 36;                       % uniform volumetric source term
TL = 40; TR = 10; TT = 30; TB = 20;   % boundary temperatures
%% Step 2: Assemble A and b using the 5-point stencil
A = sparse(N*N, N*N);
b = zeros(N*N, 1);
idx = @(i,j) (j-1)*N + i;     % node numbering: left-right, bottom-top
for j = 1:N
    for i = 1:N
        p = idx(i,j);
        A(p,p) = 4;
        b(p)   = h^2*S;
        if i > 1, A(p,idx(i-1,j)) = -1; else, b(p) = b(p) + TL; end
        if i < N, A(p,idx(i+1,j)) = -1; else, b(p) = b(p) + TR; end
        if j > 1, A(p,idx(i,j-1)) = -1; else, b(p) = b(p) + TB; end
        if j < N, A(p,idx(i,j+1)) = -1; else, b(p) = b(p) + TT; end
    end
end
%% Step 3: Solve and verify the residual
Tvec = A\b;
fprintf('Residual norm = %.2e\n', norm(A*Tvec - b))
T = reshape(Tvec, N, N);
%% Step 4: Plot
surf(T); shading interp; colorbar;
xlabel('i'); ylabel('j'); zlabel('T');
title('Poisson Equation with Internal Source');

Explicit Heat Equation FTCSConditionally Stable

The explicit Forward-Time Central-Space (FTCS) method computes every new nodal temperature directly from old values — no matrix solve needed. Its price is a strict stability limit on the time step.

FTCS scheme
\[ \frac{T_i^{n+1}-T_i^n}{\Delta t} =\alpha\frac{T_{i-1}^n-2T_i^n+T_{i+1}^n}{\Delta x^2} \] \[ \boxed{T_i^{n+1}=rT_{i-1}^n+(1-2r)T_i^n+rT_{i+1}^n}, \qquad r=\frac{\alpha\Delta t}{\Delta x^2} \]
🧮
Derivation — Von Neumann Stability Analysis
1Insert a Fourier mode

Assume an error component of the form \(T_j^n=\xi^n e^{Ikx_j}\), where \(\xi\) is the amplification factor per step. Substituting into FTCS and using \(e^{Ik\Delta x}+e^{-Ik\Delta x}=2\cos(k\Delta x)\):

\[ \xi = 1+2r\left[\cos(k\Delta x)-1\right] = 1-4r\sin^2\!\left(\frac{k\Delta x}{2}\right) \]
2Require \(|\xi|\le 1\) for every wavenumber

The worst case is \(\sin^2=1\) (the shortest wavelength the grid can hold):

\[ -1\le 1-4r\le 1 \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \boxed{r\le\frac12} \]
Equivalently: \(\Delta t\le\dfrac{\Delta x^2}{2\alpha}\). Halving \(\Delta x\) forces \(\Delta t\) down by 4×.
Stability requirement: for 1D FTCS use \(r\le 0.5\); for 2D with equal spacing, \(r_x+r_y\le 0.5\). If violated, the shortest-wavelength error mode is amplified every step and grows into a sawtooth oscillation.
⏩

Implicit Heat Equation BTCS · Backward EulerUnconditionally Stable

The implicit (BTCS) method evaluates the spatial derivative at the new time level. Each step requires a tridiagonal solve, but there is no stability restriction — any \(\Delta t\) works for the linear heat equation. The tridiagonal system is solved with the Thomas algorithm in \(O(N)\) operations.

Backward Euler in time, central difference in space
\[ \frac{T_i^{n+1}-T_i^n}{\Delta t}=\alpha\frac{T_{i-1}^{n+1}-2T_i^{n+1}+T_{i+1}^{n+1}}{\Delta x^2} \] \[ \boxed{-rT_{i-1}^{n+1}+(1+2r)T_i^{n+1}-rT_{i+1}^{n+1}=T_i^n} \]

Von Neumann analysis gives \(\xi=\dfrac{1}{1+4r\sin^2(k\Delta x/2)}\le1\) for every \(r>0\) — unconditional stability, but only first-order accuracy in time.

Thomas algorithm (TDMA) — the workhorse of implicit PDE solvers

For a tridiagonal system \(a_iT_{i-1}+b_iT_i+c_iT_{i+1}=d_i\):

Forward sweep: \(c_1'=c_1/b_1\), \(d_1'=d_1/b_1\); then for \(i=2\ldots N\): \(m=b_i-a_ic_{i-1}'\), \(c_i'=c_i/m\), \(d_i'=(d_i-a_id_{i-1}')/m\)
Back substitution: \(T_N=d_N'\); then \(T_i=d_i'-c_i'T_{i+1}\) for \(i=N-1\ldots1\)

%% Step 1: Data

        T(i) = dp(i) - cp(i)*T(i+1);

Crank–Nicolson Method Second Order in TimeUnconditionally Stable

Crank–Nicolson averages the explicit and implicit spatial operators — a trapezoidal rule in time. It is second-order accurate in both space and time and unconditionally stable, making it the default workhorse for 1D diffusion problems.

Crank–Nicolson stencil
\[ \frac{T_i^{n+1}-T_i^n}{\Delta t} =\frac{\alpha}{2}\left[ \frac{\delta_x^2 T^{n+1}}{\Delta x^2} +\frac{\delta_x^2 T^{n}}{\Delta x^2}\right] \] \[ \boxed{ -\frac r2T_{i-1}^{n+1}+(1+r)T_i^{n+1}-\frac r2T_{i+1}^{n+1} = \frac r2T_{i-1}^n+(1-r)T_i^n+\frac r2T_{i+1}^n} \]

Amplification factor: \(\xi=\dfrac{1-2r\sin^2(k\Delta x/2)}{1+2r\sin^2(k\Delta x/2)}\). Always \(|\xi|\le1\) — stable — but \(\xi\to-1\) for large \(r\), which can cause slowly-decaying oscillations when the initial data has sharp jumps.

ADI Method Alternating Direction Implicit2D Workhorse

ADI is the standard method for two-dimensional parabolic PDEs. It splits each time step into two half-steps: one implicit in x (rows), one implicit in y (columns). Every line solve is a cheap tridiagonal system — no large 2D matrix is ever formed.

2D heat equation and grid numbers
\[ \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} =\alpha\left(\frac{\partial^2T}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2T}{\partial y^2}\right), \qquad r_x=\frac{\alpha\Delta t}{\Delta x^2},\qquad r_y=\frac{\alpha\Delta t}{\Delta y^2} \]

Peaceman–Rachford splitting

1First half-step: x-implicit, y-explicit
\[ -\frac{r_x}{2}T_{i-1,j}^* +(1+r_x)T_{i,j}^* -\frac{r_x}{2}T_{i+1,j}^* = \frac{r_y}{2}T_{i,j-1}^n +(1-r_y)T_{i,j}^n +\frac{r_y}{2}T_{i,j+1}^n \]

For each fixed row \(j\), solve one tridiagonal system along x with the Thomas algorithm.

2Second half-step: y-implicit, x-explicit
\[ -\frac{r_y}{2}T_{i,j-1}^{n+1} +(1+r_y)T_{i,j}^{n+1} -\frac{r_y}{2}T_{i,j+1}^{n+1} = \frac{r_x}{2}T_{i-1,j}^* +(1-r_x)T_{i,j}^* +\frac{r_x}{2}T_{i+1,j}^* \]

For each fixed column \(i\), solve one tridiagonal system along y. The combination is second-order in time and unconditionally stable for the 2D heat equation.

Cost comparison: a fully implicit 2D step on an \(N\times N\) grid requires one \(N^2\times N^2\) solve. ADI replaces it with \(2N\) tridiagonal solves of size \(N\) — an enormous saving, since each Thomas solve is only \(O(N)\).

%% ADI (Peaceman-Rachford) method for the 2D heat equation
% Each time step = x-implicit sweep + y-implicit sweep.
% Every line solve is tridiagonal (here done with backslash for clarity).
clc; clear; close all;
%% Step 1: Grid and parameters
Nx = 20; Ny = 20;
alpha = 1;
dx = 1/(Nx+1);  dy = 1/(Ny+1);
dt = 5e-4;
rx = alpha*dt/dx^2;
ry = alpha*dt/dy^2;
T  = 100*ones(Ny,Nx);       % hot interior, cold (zero) boundaries
nt = 50;
%% Step 2: Constant sweep matrices
Ax = diag((1+rx)*ones(Nx,1)) + diag(-(rx/2)*ones(Nx-1,1),1) + diag(-(rx/2)*ones(Nx-1,1),-1);
Ay = diag((1+ry)*ones(Ny,1)) + diag(-(ry/2)*ones(Ny-1,1),1) + diag(-(ry/2)*ones(Ny-1,1),-1);
%% Step 3: Time marching
for n = 1:nt
    Tstar = zeros(Ny,Nx);
    % ---- half-step 1: x implicit, one tridiagonal solve per row ----
    for j = 1:Ny
        rhs = (1-ry)*T(j,:)';
        if j > 1,  rhs = rhs + (ry/2)*T(j-1,:)'; end
        if j < Ny, rhs = rhs + (ry/2)*T(j+1,:)'; end
        Tstar(j,:) = (Ax\rhs)';

    % ---- half-step 2: y implicit, one tridiagonal solve per column ----
    for i = 1:Nx
        rhs = (1-rx)*Tstar(:,i);
        if i > 1,  rhs = rhs + (rx/2)*Tstar(:,i-1); end
        if i < Nx, rhs = rhs + (rx/2)*Tstar(:,i+1); end
        T(:,i) = Ay\rhs;
    end
end

Method of Lines PDE → ODE SystemReuses RK4

The Method of Lines (MOL) discretizes only the spatial derivatives, producing a system of ODEs in time. Every ODE method from the companion page — RK2, RK3, RK4, implicit solvers — can then be applied directly. MOL is the natural bridge between the ODE and PDE worlds.

Heat equation transformed into an ODE system
\[ \frac{dT_i}{dt}=\alpha\frac{T_{i-1}-2T_i+T_{i+1}}{\Delta x^2}, \qquad i=1,2,\ldots,N \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \frac{d\mathbf{T}}{dt}=\frac{\alpha}{\Delta x^2}\left(\mathbf{M}\mathbf{T}+\mathbf{b}\right) \]

where \(\mathbf{M}\) is the tridiagonal \((1,-2,1)\) matrix and \(\mathbf{b}\) carries the boundary values.

Why the system becomes stiff

The eigenvalues of the semi-discrete operator are

\[ \lambda_k=-\frac{4\alpha}{\Delta x^2}\sin^2\!\left(\frac{k\pi}{2(N+1)}\right), \qquad k=1,\ldots,N \]

The stiffness ratio \(|\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}|\) grows like \(N^2\). Fine spatial grids therefore produce stiff ODE systems — explicit RK4 needs tiny time steps, while ode15s-type implicit solvers remain efficient.

Wave Equation Hyperbolic PDECFL Condition

The wave equation contains a second time derivative, so the marching scheme couples three time levels and needs a special first-step formula built from the two initial conditions.

Explicit central-difference wave scheme
\[ \frac{u_i^{n+1}-2u_i^n+u_i^{n-1}}{\Delta t^2} =c^2\frac{u_{i-1}^n-2u_i^n+u_{i+1}^n}{\Delta x^2} \] \[ \boxed{u_i^{n+1}=2(1-\lambda^2)u_i^n-u_i^{n-1}+\lambda^2(u_{i-1}^n+u_{i+1}^n)}, \qquad \lambda=\frac{c\Delta t}{\Delta x} \]
Special first step from the initial velocity

The scheme needs \(u^{-1}\), which does not exist. Use a Taylor expansion of \(u(x,\Delta t)\) with \(u(x,0)=f(x)\) and \(u_t(x,0)=g(x)\), replacing \(u_{tt}\) by \(c^2u_{xx}\):

\[ \boxed{u_i^1=u_i^0+\Delta t\,g_i+\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\left(u_{i-1}^0-2u_i^0+u_{i+1}^0\right)} \]
CFL condition: stability requires \(\lambda\le1\) — the numerical domain of dependence must contain the physical one. Information travels at speed \(c\); the grid must not be outrun.
🌊
Worked Example — Three Wave Steps with Exact Comparison

\(c=1\), \(\Delta x=0.25\), \(\Delta t=0.2\Rightarrow\lambda=0.8,\;\lambda^2=0.64\). Fixed ends, \(u(x,0)=\sin(\pi x)\), zero initial velocity.

SOR & PSOR Accelerated IterationConstraints

Successive Over-Relaxation (SOR) accelerates Gauss-Seidel by pushing each update past its Gauss-Seidel target. Projected SOR (PSOR) adds a clamp so the solution respects an inequality constraint — the standard tool for obstacle and free-boundary problems.

SOR and PSOR updates
\[ T_P^{GS}=\frac{T_E+T_W+T_N+T_S+h^2S}4 \] \[ T_P^{SOR}=(1-\omega)T_P^{old}+\omega T_P^{GS}, \qquad 0<\omega<2 \] \[ T_P^{PSOR}=\max\left(\psi_P,\;T_P^{SOR}\right) \]

Here \(\psi_P\) is the obstacle (lower bound). Without an obstacle, PSOR reduces to plain SOR.

Optimal relaxation factor for Laplace on a square
\[ \omega_{opt}=\frac{2}{1+\sin(\pi h)} \]
Example: \(h=0.05\Rightarrow\sin(\pi h)=0.1564\Rightarrow\omega_{opt}=2/1.1564=1.7295\)

SOR with \(\omega_{opt}\) reduces the iteration count from \(O(N^2)\) (Gauss-Seidel) to \(O(N)\) — a dramatic gain on fine grids.

PSOR / SOR iteration with obstacle support
%% PSOR / SOR iteration for Poisson-type PDEs with an optional obstacle
clc; clear; close all;
%% Step 1: Grid and boundaries
N = 30;
T = zeros(N+2, N+2);
T(end,:) = 100;                 % top boundary hot, others zero
%% Step 2: Relaxation settings
h = 1/(N+1);
omega = 2/(1 + sin(pi*h));      % theoretical optimum for Laplace
fprintf('Optimal omega = %.4f\n', omega)

maxIter = 10000;
%% Step 3: Obstacle (set to -inf for plain SOR, or a field for PSOR)
obstacle = 15*ones(size(T));    % try -inf(size(T)) for unconstrained SOR
%% Step 4: PSOR sweeps
for iter = 1:maxIter
    Told = T;
    for j = 2:N+1
        for i = 2:N+1
            gs  = 0.25*(T(j,i-1) + T(j,i+1) + T(j-1,i) + T(j+1,i));

            T(j,i) = max(obstacle(j,i), sor);   % projection step
        end
    end
    if max(abs(T(:) - Told(:))) < tol
        break
    end
end
